169 3.1 Inputs to the EXHYTE Cycle

170  In automated scientific discovery systems, user input ranges from natural language

171  queries about research topics to domain-specific research papers or datasets, playing a

172  central role in aligning system behavior with specific goals. High-level research direc-

173  tions, such as workshop themes, set the scope for the automated scientific workflow.

174  For example, the ICLR 2025 workshop “I Can’t Believe It’s Not Better” (ICBINB)

175  focused on real-world pitfalls, challenges, and negative or inconclusive results in deep

176  learning, a perspective incorporated in AI-Scientist-v2 [12]. Similarly, focused prob-

177  lem statements, such as ”How can we improve the energy efficiency of AI models?”

178  (e.g., SCI-IDEA [13]), guide idea and hypothesis generation. Extending this idea,

179  IRIS [14] formalizes user input as research goals that explicitly decompose into a

180  problem and its motivation/context. For example, the research goal “To improve the

181  balance between quality and diversity in text generation (Problem) by addressing

182  the limitations of existing sampling methods like top-p (nucleus sampling), which

183  often produce incoherent or repetitive outputs at higher temperatures (motivation/-

184  context)”. Structured, domain-specific inputs similarly allow users to express goals

185  and constraints in scientific formats. For example, LLMatDesign [5] begins with user-

186  specified chemical compositions and target properties for candidate materials, while

187  AutoMAT [15] accepts user-specified targets such as yield strength, density, or elemen-

188  tal cost constraints. In drug discovery, systems such as [16] operate on query molecules

189  represented as SMILES strings and PiFlow [17] integrates foundational scientific laws

190  or expert-defined principles to guide search.

191                  Beyond explicit goal specification, user-provided resources such as research papers

192  and datasets function as empirical and contextual inputs. Systems like ResearchA-

193  gent [18], CodeScientist [19], and HypER [20] leverage literature retrieval and idea

194  grounding. CodeScientist [19], for example, summarizes vetted code blocks to ensure

195  that generated research ideas are practical, implementable, and executable. Similarly,

196  datasets provide an empirical foundation for discovery. DrugMCTS [21], for example,

197  employs molecule–protein interaction datasets such as DrugBank [22] and KIBA [23]

198  to support drug discovery tasks.

199                Together, these examples highlight the pivotal role of user input, whether conveyed

200  through language, structured scientific specifications, or empirical data, in steering

201  automated discovery workflows across diverse scientific domains.

202 3.2 The Explore Stage

203 3.2.1 E1: Query Structuring

204  User query structuring is the first step in the Explore Stage, where it transforms raw,

205  unstructured research inputs into actionable representations. This process employs

206  complementary strategies including decomposition, vector representation, and infor-

207  mation extraction from datasets to enable targeted exploration across literature and

208  data (Fig. 3A and 4).

209  E1-S1: Decomposition. Decomposition transforms complex research queries into

210  tractable sub-queries/questions or tasks that enable focused literature/dataset

211  retrieval, reasoning, and solution design using LLM. Different systems operational-

212  ize decomposition at various levels. Chain of Ideas [24] decomposes initial research

213  topic into multiple sub-queries that capture diverse perspectives on the same prob-

214  lem., SCI-IDEA [13] extracts key-phrases that represent the underlying research focus

215  of a scientist’s query, Assay2Mol [25] identifies keywords from user-provided text

216  descriptions of target proteins or phenotypes, Robin [26] formulates a series of gen-

217  eral questions based on the name of the target disease that the scientist inputs into

218  the system, DrugLLM [27] decomposes molecules into structural groups to facilitate

219  reasoning about chemical properties.

220                Building on this principle, several systems employ LLM-based agents to decom-

221  pose complex user inputs—whether research queries or design tasks—into smaller,

222  manageable sub-tasks and to generate detailed, step-by-step workflow plans. General-

223  purpose frameworks such as ResearchAgent [18], AI-Researcher [28], SciAgent [29],

224  Zhou et al. [30] and domain specific system such as ProtAgent(protein design) [31],

225  AtomAgents(alloy design) [32], MatExpert(material discovery) [4],DrugAgents(drug

226  discovery) [33] all follow similar decomposition pipeline.

227  E1-S2: Vector representations. Encoding user inputs as vector representations

228  enables efficient retrieval, semantic similarity search, and context-aware reasoning.

229  Frameworks such as Chain of Ideas [24], SciPIP [34], SCI-IDEA [13], SCIMON

230  [35], IRIS [14] transform research queries, questions, background knowledge into

231  embeddings to support semantic retrieval of relevant scientific literature.

232                  In domain-specific applications, Chemist-X [1] encodes molecules as SMILES

233  strings to retrieve reaction conditions for target products, while DrugMCTS [21]

234  identifies structurally similar molecules using vectorized molecular representations.

235  Assay2Mol [25] embeds protein target descriptions and phenotypic data for BioAssay

236  retrieval, and MatExpert [4] employs dual encoders with one for property descriptions

237  and another for structural representations to improve reference material retrieval. Col-

238  lectively, these systems underscore the pivotal role of vector representations in enabling

239  semantically rich, machine-interpretable forms of knowledge that drive efficient and

240      context-aware scientific discovery

241  E1-S3: Information Extraction from Datasets. Extracting key features and

242  annotations from structured datasets enables automated systems to generate empiri-

243  cal inputs for downstream tasks such as model training, hypothesis formulation, and

244  idea generation. AstroAgents [36], for instance, employs a Data Analyst Agent to

262 3.2.2 E2: Data Retrieval

263  Retrieving literature and datasets is a foundational step in scientific discovery, ensuring

264  that exploration remains grounded in comprehensive and relevant knowledge. Guided

265  by user inputs, this E2 step compiles scientific publications and datasets that support

266   informed hypothesis exploration and validation. We identified three key strategies that

267  capture how systems retrieve information through LLM-based agents, related-work

268  networks, and scientific repositories, respectively (Fig. 3A and 4).

269  E2-S1: LLM Agent–Based Retrieval. LLM agents now routinely query and curate

270  literature and datasets. General-purpose frameworks such as Chain of Ideas [24] and

271  Graph of AI Ideas [41] use LLMs to generate multiple research queries from an initial

272  topic, capturing diverse conceptual angles. SciAgents [29] and AI-Scientist [42], along

273  with AI-Scientist-v2 [12], retrieve literature via the Semantic Scholar API for novelty

274  checks and evidence gathering.

275                LLM agents are also tasked with prioritizing the most relevant papers. IRIS [14]

276  introduces a retrieval agent that issues targeted queries answered by the specialized

277  “AI2 Scholar QA” API. Moose-Chem [2] generates titles from background papers

278  to retrieve inspirational works. AI-Researcher [28] designs a “Knowledge Acquisition

279  Agent” that, given reference papers, retrieves additional papers and code repositories

280  from scientific databases.

281                In domain-specific settings, retrieval is coupled with scientific reasoning. DrugA-

282  gent [33] employs an “Instructor Agent” to query domain libraries and translate

283  abstract ideas into executable code via domain-specific examples. AtomAgents [32]

284  integrates a specialized knowledge-retrieval tool that extracts material properties

285  directly from published papers. Chemist-X [1] produces executable code to interrogate

286  repositories such as PubChem for chemical literature and molecular datasets, while

287  ProtAgents [31] retrieves specific data points (e.g., Protein Data Bank (PDB) identi-

288  fiers) for protein design. In materials discovery, AutoMAT [15] uses LLMs, together

289  with user-defined property targets and constraints, to consult reference handbooks

290  (e.g., titanium alloy databases) and identify candidates meeting elemental criteria.

291  E2-S2: Related-Work Based Retrieval. Citation networks and semantic similar-

292  ity are widely used to link related works and surface new directions. ResearchAgent

293  [18], SCIMON [35], HypER [20], and Chain of Ideas [24] expand from an anchor paper

294  by retrieving both citing and cited works to capture the evolution of research themes;

295  HypER further adds LLM-based relevancy scoring. AI-Researcher [28] incorporates

296  multi-step reference selection for each target paper, including citation-pattern analy-

297   sis (frequency), context analysis (influence on methods/theory/experiments), evidence

298  collection (textual support), and an integrated impact score. SciPIP [34] combines

299  keyword, co-occurrence, and semantics-based retrieval, then applies a cluster-based

300  selection of representative papers. SCI-IDEA [13] queries academic sources using key

301  phrases drawn from a researcher’s work and similar literature. Scideator [43] retrieves

302  multiple analogs per source paper, uses an LLM to generate “purpose–mechanism”

303  pairs, and then issues queries per pair to find additional relevant papers. Domain-

304  focused approaches tailor queries to specialized terms. For example, ProteinHypothesis

305  [38] uses domain-specific keywords (e.g., “Protein Science”) to retrieve metadata

306  (titles, abstracts, links); AstroAgents [36] selects relevant papers and books to pro-

307  vide astrobiology context; and Tong et al. [44] require the presence of terms such as

308  “psychol,” “clin psychol,” and “biol psychol” in titles or abstracts for psychology/neu-

309  roscience. Manual and semi-automatic curation complements these methods via direct

310  selection from leading databases (e.g., Sparks of Science [45], Perovskite-R1 [46], Bud-

311  get AI Researcher [47], Lee et al. [16], Quilodr´an-Casas et al. [48], Cheng et al. [49],

312  Ciucua et al. [50]).

313  E2-S3: Dataset Retrieval from Scientific Repositories. Datasets provide the

314  empirical foundation for analysis, model training, and validation. In drug discov-

315  ery, DrugMCTS [21] queries databases to retrieve molecules structurally similar to

316  a query compound, associated protein interactors, and binding-pocket information

317  parsed from Protein Data Bank (PDB) files to improve LLM interpretability. DrugA-

318  gent [33] retrieves biological datasets from the Therapeutics Data Commons (TDC),

319  which offers structured, AI-ready datasets and benchmarks across drug development

320  stages. In materials discovery, FlowLLM [51] retrieves inorganic crystalline materi-

321  als from the Materials Project for LLM fine-tuning, and Perovskite-R1 [46] assembles

322  drug-like compounds from a comprehensive library to enhance generalization across

323  chemical space. MatExpert [4] and LLMatDesign [5] access NOMAD [39] and the

324  Materials Project [40] for reference materials used in training, evaluation, and mod-

325  ification workflows. Zhou et al. [30] retrieve crystal structures with compositions or

326  reduced formulas similar to a query from structural databases to identify promising

327  crystal prototypes.

328 3.2.3 E3: Knowledge Assembly

329  The knowledge-assembly stage transforms unstructured scientific text into structured,

330  queryable representations that enable efficient reasoning during discovery. The existing

331  literature typically employs three strategies: structured extraction and summarization

332  from literature, knowledge-graph construction, and database construction for large-

333  scale retrieval and synthesis(Fig. 3A and 4).

334  E3-S1: Structured extraction and summarization from literature To make

335  literature readily usable for idea and hypothesis generation, systems extract and sum-

336  marize standardized sections (e.g., titles, abstracts, methods, results). SciPIP [34],

337  Chain of Ideas [24], SCI-IDEA [13], and AstroAgents [36] implement pipelines that

338  segment papers into consistent fields. Papers like SciPIP [34], AtomAgents [32], Robin

339   [26], Literature Meets Data [52], AI-co-scientist [53] and IRIS [14] further produce con-

340  cise synopses of key findings. Sparks of Science [45] uses LLMs to extract each paper’s

341  conventional assumption and innovative approach alongside a concise insight summary

342   from abstracts. Several systems including SCI-IDEA [13], Scideator [43], SCIMON [35],

343  and Chen et al. [54] extract facets such as objectives, methods, evaluation, and future

344  work. Domain-tailored extraction includes causal relations in psychology (Tong et al.

345  [44]), question–answer pairs for Perovskite precursors (Perovskite-R1 [46]), frequent-

346  topic mining to guide retrieval (Lee et al. [47]), structured tables of target properties,

347   related structures, and linking mechanisms (Beyond Designer’s Knowledge [55]), pars-

348  ing mechanical properties and compositions to propose alloys (AutoMAT [15]), and

349  sentence-level evidence selection for property-optimization queries (dziner [56]).

350  E3-S2: Knowledge graph construction. Knowledge graphs (KGs) encode entities

351  and relations to support structured reasoning and discovery. SCIMON [35] represents

352  tasks, methods, materials, and metrics as nodes, with “used-for” edges to ground

353  generated ideas. Tong et al. [44] construct causal KGs in psychology, where (cause,

354  effect) pairs are directed nodes with attributes (interpretations) and relation types

355  (causal vs. correlational). Y-Mol [57] builds a biomedical KG with interactions among

356  drugs, genes, and diseases (Drug–Target, Drug–Drug, and Disease–Gene). ResearchA-

357  gent [18] assembles an entity-centric store from co-occurrences in literature to expose

358  cross-domain connections (topics, keywords, individuals, events). SciPIP [34] links

359  papers to LLM-extracted keywords in a paper–keyword graph for efficient keyword-

360  based retrieval. Graph of AI Ideas [41] constructs a paper-level graph using structured

361  quadruples (paper, citation position, citation semantics, paper), where citation posi-

362  tion captures reference importance and citation semantics defines the purpose of each

363  citation.

364  E3-S3: Database construction. Curated databases consolidate heterogeneous

365  resources into structured repositories for retrieval, analysis, and synthesis. SciPIP [34]

366  builds a literature database from top venues over the past decade, storing key sections

367  and re-summarizing each paper into a quintuple (keywords, background, ideas, concise

368  methods, core references), with background/ideas encoded as embeddings. ResearchA-

369  gent [18] extracts entities from titles/abstracts (e.g., post-2023 Semantic Scholar)

370  and encodes their co-occurrences in a sparse matrix. HypER [20] compiles temporal

371  reasoning-chain datasets linked to related papers for effective reasoning over scien-

372  tific literature. SCIMON [35] builds corpora from the ACL Anthology and PubMed,

373  organizing the papers with extracted (Background, Target) pairs, seed terms, and

374  their relations. Sparks of Science [45] constructs the “HypoGen” dataset by extracting

375  assumptions, innovations, and core insights from abstracts. ProteinHypothesis [38],

376  Lee et al. [47], and Cheng et al. [49] store vector embeddings of papers from arXiv

377  and major conferences (e.g., NeurIPS, ICLR) to support scalable retrieval.

378 3.3 The Hypothesize Stage

379 3.3.1 H1: Hypothesis/Idea Generation

380  Hypothesis and idea generation mark the transition from structured knowledge to

381  novel discovery, synthesizing literature, data, and reasoning strategies into creative,

382  testable hypotheses. We identify four strategies commonly employed in the literature,

383  including zero-shot generation, multi-agent generation, literature-based generation,

384  and data-driven generation(Fig. 3B and 4).

385  H1-S1: Zero-shot generation. Zero-shot generation relies on the internal knowledge

386  encoded in LLMs, thus enabling idea/hypothesis creation without explicit external

387  context—useful when domain-specific training data are limited. Frameworks illustrat-

388  ing this approach include LLMatDesign [5], Buehler et al. [58], Tang et al. [59], Yin

389  et al. [60], Bystronski et al. [61], Abdel et al. [62], Rabby et al. [63], Guo et al. [64],

390  Pu et al. [17], Liu et al. [65], and Manning et al. [66].

391  H1-S2: Multi-agent generation. Multi-agent frameworks build specialized agents,

392  coordinated under an orchestrator agent to produce and refine hypotheses. AstroA-

393  gents [36] employs a hierarchical design, where a ‘Data Analysis Agent’ first uncovers

394  key patterns and trends and a ‘Planner Agent’ delegates distinct data segments to

395  multiple ‘Scientist Agents’ for in-depth exploration and investigation; finally each sci-

396  entist operates within its assigned domain to produce novel hypotheses, which are

397  consolidated by an ’Accumulator Agent’ that deduplicates and integrates the results.

398  In the KG-grounded SciAgents [29], an Assistant Agent drafts an initial subgraph to

399  contextualize the idea, an Ontologist Agent defines nodes and interprets edges, and

400  two Scientist Agents iteratively draft, elaborate, and critique detailed hypotheses.

401  Robin [26] specializes in biomedicine and designed an agent ’Crow’ to identify causal

402  disease mechanisms and suitable in vitro models and another agent ’Falcon’ to con-

403  duct deep literature review to evaluate therapeutic candidates. In chemical discovery,

404  DrugMCTS [21] coordinates multiple sequential agents to incrementally build reason-

405  ing paths for protein target identification, where a ’Molecule-Analysis’ Agent produces

406   natural-language molecular reports, a ’Molecule-Selection Agent’ filters candidates, an

407  ’Interaction-Analysis’ Agent explores molecule–protein interactions, and a ’Decision’

408  Agent synthesizes upstream findings to predict promising targets.

409  H1-S3: Literature-based generation. Literature-driven approaches retrieve, syn-

410  thesize, and recombine knowledge from scientific texts to propose novel hypotheses.

411  ResearchAgent [18] and Moose-Chem [2] provide titles/abstracts of various related

412  papers as context for LLM. Genesys [49] formulates new LLM design ideas by modi-

413  fying parent designs from the Knowledge Engine (KE), which integrates the curated

414  literature with code and external sources. Similarly, dziner [56] generates modifications

415  based on retrieved material-design guidelines mined from unstructured text (e.g. sci-

416  entific literature and the Internet). Another common strategy is summarization-based

417  synthesis, where relevant sections are distilled and restructured before prompting the

418  LLM (e.g., AI-co-scientist [53], SciPIP [34], ProteinHypothesis [38], Perovskite-R1

419  [46]). Another approach is facet recombination, in which facets (e.g. purpose and mech-

420  anism) are recombined to surface gaps and generate ideas. For example, Scideator [43]

421  constructs analogies from purpose–mechanism pairs across papers; SCI-IDEA [13] ana-

422  lyzes similar facets to detect patterns, inconsistencies, and unexplored areas; Liu et al.

423  [55] and Lee et al. [47] synthesize novel interdependencies among mechanisms or top-

424  ics not explicitly linked in the source literature; and CODESCIENTIST [19] explores

425  combinatorial idea generation via genetic operators (e.g., crossover, mutation). A com-

426  plementary line of work is a reasoning-chain–based generation, which reconstructs

427  explanatory pathways from the noisy/fragmented literature. HypER [20], Sparks of

428  Science [45], and Graph of AI Ideas [41] fall into this line and identify valid chains

429  in the literature graphs and use them as scaffolds for evidence-grounded hypotheses.

430  Finally, KGs are proposed to structure scientific concepts and relations for systematic

431  hypothesis generation. For example, Tong et al. [44] employ link prediction algorithms

432  on causal knowledge graphs to identify high-probability causal relationships among

433  previously unconnected concepts.; Lee et al. [16] integrate biochemical drug–drug rela-

434  tionships from a KG to interpret the network structure, then pass these summaries

435  to a prediction agent; Gao et al. [67] generate plausible logical hypotheses from KG

436  entity sets.

437  H1-S4: Data-driven generation. Structured datasets provide a complementary

438  source, where data instances and patterns can guide hypothesis formation. One group

439  of systems detects patterns and supplies them as context to the LLM. In AstroAgents

440  [36], a ’Data Analyst’ Agent examines mass spectrometry to uncover PAH(Polycyclic

441  Aromatic Hydrocarbons) distributions and alkylation patterns, highlighting unex-

442  pected findings. ProteinHypothesis [38] analyzes sequence motifs, secondary-structure

443  correlations, and functional-site patterns from experimental data. Proteus [37] runs

444   targeted statistical analyses over multi-omics data using bioinformatics tools, recording

445   quantified relationships between biological entities (e.g. proteins, transcripts, variants)

446  and clinical features (e.g. survival time, tumor stage) as edges in a ’Conclusion Graph’

447  that then grounds LLM hypothesis generation. Another line seeds the LLM with few-

448  shot data instances: HypoGeniC [68], Literature Meets Data [52], and Hypotheses for

449  Inductive Reasoning [69] provide small sets of (input, label) pairs to induce initial

450  hypotheses. In DrSR [70], ChatSR [71], and LLM-Feynman [6], observational pairs

451  guide LLM in analyzing structural relationships, which are then used by symbolic

452  regression to produce interpretable formulas. Zheng et al. [72] discover novel molecular

453  patterns using structural/chemical features and then use them to predict molecular

454  properties. Liu et al. [73] retrieve domain-specific drug data as demonstrations for LLM

455  to perform drug-editing tasks. In material, MAPPS [30] queries structural databases

456  for crystal prototypes with compositions/reduced formulas similar to a query, using

457   them to generate initial candidate structures; MatExpert [4] fine-tunes a small LLM on

458  curated transition pathways and target materials from NOMAD to generate plausible

459  transitions pathways. In healthcare and clinical design, Zhu et al. [74] and Bani et al.

460  [75] fine-tune LLMs on patent–title pairs or clinical datasets containing various clinical

461  scenario and ground-truth diagnoses to enable new design ideas and hypotheses.

462 3.3.2 H2: Hypothesis or Idea Prioritization

463  Hypothesis prioritization focuses analytical and computational resources on the most

464  promising ideas by evaluating their novelty, plausibility, feasibility, and potential

465  impact. We identify four common strategies: LLM-based evaluation, literature-based

466  assessment, quantitative assessment using domain metrics, and human evaluation(Fig.

467  3B and 4).

468  H2-S1: LLM-based evaluation. LLM agents can act as evaluators, ranking

469  ideas/hypotheses on clarity, feasibility, novelty, plausibility, consistency, and impact.

470   Exemples include ResearchAgent [18],AstroAgents [36], SciAgents [29], AI-co-scientist

471  [53], Sparks of Science [45], Moose-chem [2], Tong et al [44], PROTEUS [37], Rabby et

472  al [63], ProteinHypothesis [38]. Robin [26] uses an LLM judge to rank drug candidates

473  by scientific rationale, pharmacological profile, and methodology of the support-

474  ing literature. AstroAgents [36] uses a critic agent to assess the alignment between

475  hypotheses and observational data. CLADD [16] evaluates drugs by predicting its

476  toxicity from molecule’s chemical representation(SMILES), infers likely protein tar-

477  gets (mechanism of action) from LLM agent generated comprehensive report on query

478  molecule, and property categorization performance of molecules based on generated

479  textual description concatenated with its SMILES representation. DrugMCTS [21]

480  extends this by integrating LLM-based reward functions within a Monte Carlo Tree

481  Search framework to score molecular–protein interactions using structural (binding

482  pocket) and literature-derived contextual evidence. ChatBattery [76] uses a ’Search’

483  Agent to verify novelty by querying the Materials Project database and a ’Retrieval’

484   Agent to identify similar lithium-based compounds from the ICSD, guiding subsequent

485  hypothesis revisions. Liu et al [55] evaluate materials design hypotheses based on the

486  alignment between the stated materials design goals and the content of the hypoth-

487  esis. LLM-Feynman [6] employs an LLM to assign ’interpretability score’ to its own

488  generated formulas, quantifying their physical and chemical meaning and ProtAgents

489  [31] assess success in generating proteins with desired secondary structures.

490  H2-S2: Literature-based assessment. To ensure that generated hypotheses are

491  both novel and scientifically grounded, many systems incorporate literature-based

492  assessment as a critical step. This process involves retrieving relevant prior work, eval-

493  uating semantic similarity, and determining whether the proposed idea contributes to

494  new insights. Systems such as Scideator [43], HypER [20], SCIMON [35], AI-scientist

495  [42], AI-Scientist-v2 [12], AstroAgents [36], Graph of AI Ideas [41], SCI-IDEA [13],

496  SciAgents [29], Budget AI Researcher [47], Cheng et al [49], Lee et al [47] deploy LLM-

497  based ‘Novelty Checker’, which assesses the idea’s novelty against relevant papers.

498   HypER [20] evaluates whether the rationale and hypothesis track the logic and chronol-

499  ogy of literature chains. CtrlHGen [67] measures the alignment between hypothesis

500  conclusions and the observed entities in the knowledge graph using multiple simi-

501  larity metrics. These metrics are incorporated into the reward function that guides

502  hypothesis generation.

503  H2-S3: Quantitative Assessment Using Domain Metrics. Quantitative eval-

504  uation using domain-specific scientific metrics and computational models provides

505  an objective measure of hypothesis. dziner [56] calculate ’synthesizability’ (e.g. syn-

506  thetic accessibility score, quantitative estimate of drug-likeness (QED score)) using

507   PubChem-scale molecule dataset and specialized tools. The effectiveness of a proposed

508  modification is then evaluated with domain-expert predictive models, and the LLM

509  ultimately decides whether to accept or reject the modification based on these quanti-

510  tative results. Matexpert [4] evaluates generated material structural validity, chemical

511  feasibility and stability using quantitative and physics-informed metrics. ChatBat-

512  tery [76] verifies the stability and properties of the top-ranked cathode candidates

513  using a machine learning surrogate model(MACE-MP) that predicts the energies and

514  forces of the sampled structures. MAPPS [30] evaluates crystal structures for struc-

515  tural quality, thermodynamic stability, novelty, and accuracy. Liu et al [73] compare

516  edited molecules with desired properties, quantify binding affinity for peptide editing

517  and secondary structural content for protein secondary structure editing relative to

518  desired targets. LLMatdesign [5] predicts properties via a Machine Learning Property

519  Predictor (MLPP) after a Machine-Learning-Force-Field (MLFF) based relaxation

520  of modified material structures, declaring success when predicted properties of the

521  new material align with target specifications. In symbolic reasoning and equation dis-

522  covery, DrSR [70] and ChatSR [71] iteratively improve model output by assessing

523  generated equations using numerical fitness scores and domain performance metrics.

524  LLM-Feynman [6] provides a comprehensive quantitative evaluation framework using

525  the Coefficient of Determination and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for regression tasks,

526  and accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and cross-entropy loss for classification-based

527  symbolic modeling.

528  H2-S4: Human evaluation. Expert review complements automated scoring with

529  contextual judgment and domain nuance. Chain of Ideas [24], Tong et al [44],

530  AstroAgents [36], SciPIP [34], SciMON [35], CODESCIENTIST [19], SCI-IDEA [13],

531  Literature Meets Data [52], ChatBattery [76], Rabby et al [63] and HypER [20]

532  incorporate expert feedback to refine, rank, or filter hypotheses.

533 3.4 The Test Stage

534 3.4.1 T1: Experimental Design Generation

535  Experimental design generation translates top-ranked ideas and hypotheses into

536  verifiable experimental plans, creating a systematic path to validate or refute scien-

537  tific propositions. It comprises three common strategies: literature-informed design,

538  LL-based design generation, and test execution(Fig. 3C and 4).

539  T1-S1: Literature-informed experimental design. This strategy leverages prior

540  studies and domain references to ground experimental plans in established scientific

541  practices while enabling innovation. Robin [26] selects top-ranked ’in vitro’ models

542   from the literature review to define therapeutic testing strategies and generates concise

543   experimental outlines. ResearchAgent [18] synthesizes problem information, methods,

544  and existing designs from the literature into new protocols that emphasizing clarity,

545  robustness, reproducibility, validity, and feasibility. Chain of Ideas [24] uses the few-

546  shot prompting with examples of prior experiments, together with user-provided ideas

547  and key entities, to guide design generation. Similarly, DrugAgent [33] constructs

548   executable code from curated documentation, compiled from widely used libraries and

549  models from the literature. Together, these approaches ensure the design evidence-

550  based while adaptable to novel scientific contexts.

551  T1-S2: LLM-based experiment design generation. Beyond literature grounding,

552  systems increasingly rely on LLM reasoning to autonomously generate experimental

553  designs, often producing executable workflows or code for validation. AI-Scientist-v2

554  [12] employs ’Agentic Tree Search algorithm’ to systematically explore hypotheses

555  during the experimentation phase. For each node in the experimental pathway, an

556  LLM proposes a concrete experimentation plan and the Python code to implement it.

557  Similarly, AI-researcher [28], Lee et al [69], Lui et al [65], Cheng et al [49] transform

558  research analyses and development plans into executable code via LLM agents. In

559  physics-based discovery, Zhou et al [30] invoke tools from a physics toolbox to translate

560  workflow steps into executable, physically grounded Python code. CODESCIENTIST

561  [19] uses a ’planer agent’ that ingests the idea, expert comments, and a codeblock

562   library to produce a concrete plan and required codeblocks. AI-co-scientist [53] features

563   a ’Generation Agent’ that proposes experimental protocols for downstream validations.

564  In biomedical experimental setting, ProteinHypothesis [38] assesses the experimental

565  feasibility of hypotheses and maps the hypotheses to laboratory techniques, while

566  Chemist-X [1] generates control scripts for an agent to operate wet-lab platforms for

567  validation.

568  T1-S3: Test execution. This strategy validates LLM-generated hypotheses via in

569  silico or wet-lab experiments, closing the discovery loop by determining the real-

570  world feasibility and accuracy of these hypotheses. In Robin [26], the experimental

571  strategies are proposed for validating therapeutic candidates by identifying a rele-

572  vant disease mechanism and in vitro models and executed by human researchers in an

573  iterative lab-in-the-loop framework. In clinical hypothesis refinement, LA-CDM [75]

574  uses a ’decision agent’ to request additional diagnostic tests to reduce uncertainty.

575  Perovskite-R1 [46] implements recommended protocols and fabricates complete solar

576  cell devices to assess the efficacy of the selected additives. ChatBattery [76] synthesizes

577  and tests cathode candidates through wet-lab experiments. Chemist-X [1] validates

578  generated reaction conditions through fully automated wet-lab experiments under

579  LLM supervision. Abdel et al [62] test LLM-proposed drug combinations in the lab.

580                Execution also includes computational validation. Tang et al [59] apply regression-

581   based statistical models to test hypotheses about LLM’s explanatory power for societal

582  outcomes. Genesys [49] evaluates the proposed language model design by verify-

583  ing code correctness, conducting training-time experiments, and measuring success

584  via a multi-scale fitness function. CODESCIENTIST [19] follows an iterative gener-

585  ate–execute–reflect debugging loop, while AI-Scientist [28] and AI-Scientist-v2 [12]

586  test LLM-generated experiment prototypes by running associated Python code to test

587  conceptual validity.

588 3.4.2 T2: Iterative refinement

589  Iterative refinement improves the quality and feasibility of hypotheses, ideas, and

590  experimental designs by incorporating feedback from agents, data, and experts,

591  ensuring responsive to new information and evaluations. There are three common

592  strategies: agent feedback–guided refinement, data-driven refinement, and human

593  feedback–guided refinement(Fig. 3C and 4).

594  T2-S1: Agent feedback–guided refinement. Systems employ feedback loops in

595  which LLM-based agents iteratively revise hypotheses, code, and designs to improve

596  feasibility and correctness. AI-Scientist-v2 [12] refines algorithm nodes through agent

597   feedback by evaluating parent nodes’ outcome, where buggy parents trigger debugging

598   using clind nodes, while non-buggy parents prompt experimental refinement. Key met-

599  rics (e.g., loss values, training data) are logged and visualized and a Vision–Language

600  Model (VLM) then critiques plots for label clarity, legend completeness, and data

601  accuracy. Nodes flagged by the VLM are marked buggy, whereas validated nodes are

602  retained, enabling continuous, feedback-driven improvement. Similarly, AI-researcher

603  [28] utilizes an ’Advisor’ Agent to propose actionable refinement spanning implemen-

604  tation details, validation studies, visualizations, and comparative analyses. In clinical

605  settings, the ’Hypothesis’ Agent in LA-CDM [75] updates diagnoses and confidence

606  estimates as patient-state information evolves, repeating until a final diagnosis is

607  produced.

608  T2-S2: Data-driven refinement. Data-driven refinement keeps hypotheses, models,

609  and experimental plans empirically grounded by updating them with new compu-

610  tational and experimental evidence. In Robin [26], human scientists upload raw or

611  semi-processed data (e.g., flow cytometry, RNA-seq data), which are processed by the

612  ’Finch’ analysis agent with the requested analysis to generate statistical tests, differ-

613  ential gene expression plots. Robin also distills actionable insights from the analysis

614  results and proposes follow-up assays. Fully automated systems such as Chemist-X

615  [1] execute LLM-generated reaction conditions with closed-loop wet-lab experiments

616  and quantify reaction outcomes (e.g., the product yield using High-Performance Liq-

617  uid Chromatography). Abdel et al. [62] validate LLM-generated drug combinations

618  experimentally and compute Highest Single Agent (HSA) synergy scores to quantify

619  performance. In materials science, ChatBattery [76] characterizes physical/structural

620  performance with power conversion efficiency, film quality, and stability. Perovskite-

621  R1 [46] assess the efficacy of the selected additives by fabricating complete solar cell

622  devices and evaluating metrics such as Power Conversion Efficiency (PCE), film qual-

623  ity, and long-term device performance. In social science, Tang et al [59] convert natural

624  language hypotheses into interpretable embeddings used as explanatory variables in

625  a linear regression model to predict societal outcomes. Coefficients are tested for sig-

626  nificance, non-significant hypotheses are pruned, and the feedback guides subsequent

627  LLM proposals.

628  T2-S2: Human feedback guided refinement. Expert review adds domain-

629  specific judgment that complements automated evaluations. In ChatBattery [76],

630  human researchers revise hypotheses after examining experimental outcomes, align-

631  ing hypotheses with objectives and constraints when results indicate limited validity

632  or feasibility. CODESCIENTIST [19] generates concise reports indicating whether

633  each hypothesis was confirmed, rejected, or inconclusive, and final expert screening

634  substantially narrows the candidate set.

635 4 End-to-End Workflow

636  The previous sections examined how individual studies address specific stages and

637  substages of the EXHYTE cycle, providing a granular understanding of each method-

638  ological contribution. However, this stage-by-stage analysis does not fully reveal how

639  these strategies can be integrated into an end-to-end scientific discovery workflow. To

640  illustrate how the surveyed components interact in practice, we present two compre-

641  hensive systems: AI-Researcher and AI Co-Scientist. These case studies demonstrate

642  how strategies from the EXHYTE framework combine to enable autonomous or semi-

643  autonomous discovery, from exploration and hypothesis generation to testing and

644  refinement.

645 4.1 AI-Researcher: an in silico workflow

646  The goal of the AI-Researcher system is to design a complete research pipeline with

647  minimal human intervention capable of systematically targeting the conceptual fron-

648  tiers of science, deliberately seeking conceptual gaps, contradictory findings, and

649  emerging patterns across literature and implementations. It begins with a curated col-

650  lection of approximately 15–20 reference papers, along with optional datasets, that

651  form the foundation of knowledge for the system. These sources mirror the intellectual

652  basis of a target publication, providing the conceptual and methodological context

653  from which new discoveries emerge.

654  Explore. During the exploration stage, AI-researcher establishes the technical and

655  conceptual groundwork for autonomous algorithm design. This process is jointly

656  managed by the Knowledge Acquisition Agent and the Resource Analyst Agent, sup-

657  ported by their respective sub-agents. The Resource Analyst Agent decomposes the

658  research objective into atomic academic concepts, which are fundamental, indivisible

659  elements requiring targeted investigation. Guided by these concepts, the Knowl-

660  edge Acquisition Agent retrieves relevant papers and code repositories from scientific

661  databases, applying multi-criteria filtering to select high-quality sources (E2-S1/S2).

662  The Paper Analyst sub-agent extracts mathematical formulations from LaTeX files

663  using a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) approach (E3-S1), while the Code Ana-

664  lyst identifies corresponding code implementations, establishing explicit bidirectional

665  mappings between theory and code (E3-S3). The resulting structured report provides

666  the empirical and theoretical foundation for downstream reasoning.

667  Hypothesize. Building on the knowledge assembled during exploration, the Idea

668  Generator identifies conceptual gaps, contradictory findings, and emerging patterns

669  across literature and implementations (H1-S3). It employs a Divergent–Convergent

670  Discovery Framework: the divergent phase produces multiple conceptually distinct

671  research directions, while the convergent phase evaluates them using criteria such

672  as novelty, soundness, and transformative potential (H2-S1/S2). The Advisor Agent

673  provides expert-style feedback linking theoretical concepts to implementation plans,

674  embodying the human-in-the-loop evaluation principle (H2-S4).

675  Test. The testing phase operationalizes selected hypotheses through experimen-

676  tal planning, implementation, and iterative refinement. The Plan Agent generates

677  a comprehensive development plan, including dataset selection, training/testing

678  methodology, and evaluation metrics (T1-S1). The Code Agent translates this plan

679   into executable implementations, verifying dataset usage and experimental consistency

680  (T1-S2). Results are analyzed by the Experiment Analysis Agent, which recommends

681  code modifications and further experimentation (T2-S1). Finally, the Automated Doc-

682  umentation Agent revises the manuscript using an academic checklist to ensure clarity,

683  reproducibility, and completeness (T2-S3). Together, these stages demonstrate a con-

684  tinuous feedback loop linking hypothesis generation to empirical validation within a

685  coherent in silico research pipeline.

686 4.2 AI Co-Scientist: a wet-lab–integrated workflow

687  The primary goal of the AI-scientist is to accelerate the process of scientific discov-

688  ery by uncovering new, original knowledge operating within a ”scientist-in-the-loop”

689  collaborative paradigm. AI Co-Scientist implements the EXHYTE framework with

690  physical experimentation by coupling LLM reasoning with wet-lab validation. The sys-

691  tem accepts multiple input types from the human scientist, most critically a research

692  goal, which may range from concise statements to long documents containing numerous

693  prior publications.

694  Explore. Upon receiving the research goal, AI Co-Scientist parses it into a research

695  plan configuration specifying desired outputs, constraints, and evaluation criteria (E1-

696  S1). This configuration serves as a blueprint for downstream hypothesis generation.

697  The Generation Agent then explores relevant literature via web search and retrieval

698  tools (E2-S1/S2) to identify promising directions for novel findings.

699  Hypothesize. The Generation Agent grounds its reasoning in summarized prior work

700  and produces hypotheses and research plans (H1-S3). A Reflection Agent simulates a

701  scientific peer-review process, critically examining each hypothesis for correctness, nov-

702  elty, and evidential grounding (H2-S2). Quick reviews are followed by comprehensive

703  evaluations that integrate external search and evidence retrieval to assess validity. For

704  complex hypotheses, deep verification decomposes each claim into constituent assump-

705  tions, which are independently examined to detect invalid components. The Ranking

706  Agent employs an Elo-style tournament framework to compare hypotheses through

707  multi-turn debates, scoring them on novelty, correctness, and testability (H2-S1). The

708  top-ranked hypotheses are iteratively refined by an Evolution Agent (T2-S1/S3).

709  Test. The testing stage connects LLM-generated hypotheses to real-world valida-

710  tion. AI Co-Scientist has been deployed in several biomedical domains, demonstrating

711  end-to-end discovery. Notably, the system generated the hypothesis that chromo-

712  somal phage-inducible chromosomal islands (cf-PICIs) hijack phage tails to expand

713  host range, which was experimentally validated [77]. This “tail-hijacking” mechanism

714  revealed a previously unknown route for horizontal gene transfer in bacteria, advanc-

715  ing understanding of antimicrobial resistance evolution. This case exemplifies how

716  EXHYTE-integrated reasoning—spanning literature exploration, hypothesis genera-

717  tion, prioritization, and refinement—can produce experimentally verified discoveries.

718                Together, AI-Researcher and AI Co-Scientist illustrate how EXHYTE strategies

719  interconnect across modalities. In both, Explore stages ground the system in literature

720  and data; Hypothesize stages generate and prioritize testable ideas; and Test stages

721   operationalize and refine them through computation or experimentation. These end-to-

722  end workflows demonstrate the practical synthesis of the modular strategies surveyed

723  earlier, showing how autonomous systems can orchestrate the full scientific discovery

724  process from exploration to empirical validation.
