‌Tools and Datasets for Building EXHYTE Workflows

The preceding sections surveyed strategies for each EXHYTE stage. Here we comple- ment that view with the practical tools, APIs, and datasets that enable those strategies to operate. We group resources by their role in the cycle and note where they most naturally plug into EXHYTE.

‌Core tools and APIs

Literature & data access (E2). For scientific publications and metadata, commonly used resources include Semantic Scholar[1], PubMed[2], arXiv[3], OpenAlex[4], NASA ADS[5]. Chemical and materials data are typically sourced from PubChem[6], Materials Project[7], and the Amazon EC2 API[8].

Text parsing, extraction & normalization (E3). A variety of tools are employed for text parsing, extraction and normalization. PDF and document content are commonly processed using PyPDF2[9], SciPDF[10], pymupdf4llm[11], and S2ORC-doc2json[12], while RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter[13] is used for document chunking. HTML content is parsed with BeautifulSoup[14]. For entity extraction and normalization, tools such as BLINK[15], the NER[16] tool, PL-Marker[17], SciCo[18], and ScispaCy[19] are frequently applied.

Vector representations, similarity search & retrieval (E2/E3). A range of embedding generation methods are used to represent text, structured data, molecular properties, and knowledge graph nodes. Text embeddings are com- monly produced using models such as Text-Embedding-3 (OpenAI)[20], BERT[21], SciBERT[22], SentenceBERT[23] (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5[24], Google-GenerativeAIEmbeddings[25], and jina-embedding-v3[26]. Molecular simi- larity is computed using measures such as the Tanimoto coefficient[27] based on fingerprints and ChemBERTa[54] cosine similarity from last hidden state embeddings. For efficient similarity search and retrieval, vector indices and nearest neighbor search libraries such as FAISS[28] are widely used, supporting fast retrieval of semantically or chemically similar items.

Cheminformatics and molecular analysis (H2). Several specialized tools such as RDKit[29] for molecular feature calculation, OmegaFold[30] for protein folding pre- diction, and SynergyFinder 3.0[31] for evaluating drug synergy and additivity from experimental data.




‌Benchmark datasets and evaluation corpora

Drug Discovery, Bioactivity, and Clinical: Several benchmark datasets are widely used in drug discovery and clinical research. DrugBank[32] and KIBA[33] pro- vide molecular and bioactivity data for drug-target interaction prediction, while DAVIS[34] offers drug and protein binding affinity data. Large-scale compound collec- tions such as ZINC[35] (over 230 million purchasable 3D compounds) and ChEMBL[36] (bioactive compounds with properties) are commonly used for training, activity opti- mization, and modification tasks. Protein-ligand docking performance is assessed using

CrossDocked2020[37], and clinical evaluation often relies on MIMIC-CDM[38], which contains 2,400 patients with four abdominal conditions including patient histories, physical exam notes, imaging reports, and laboratory results.

Materials Science and Chemistry: In materials science, datasets such as MP-20 (a subset of Materials Project[7] with 45,231 inorganic crystalline materials), NOMAD[39] (2.9 million materials), Matbench[40], and JARVIS-DFT[41] (61,541 crystal structures with bandgap values) are commonly used for property prediction and generative modeling. OC20-S2EF 2M[42] provides two million catalyst structures for training models such as CatGPT. Additional chemistry-focused benchmarks include TOMATO-Chem[43], for hypothesis rediscovery and ranking from annotated papers, and Superconductor Optimization (SPO)[44], which contains 26,321 superconducting materials with chemical formulas and critical temperatures.

Auxiliary Multi-domain Testbeds: Various multi-domain datasets are used for evaluation in NLP, AI reasoning, and behavioral tasks. BUSINESS SHOES[45] evaluates binary classification for shoe suitability, and DECEPTIVE REVIEWS[46] contains genuine and fake hotel reviews for deception detection. HEADLINE POPULARITY[47] and TWEET POPULARITY[48] assess popularity prediction of headlines and tweets, respectively, while DREADDIT[49] and PERSUASIVE PAIRS[50] focus on mental stress detection and persuasive argument prediction. Systematic review and reasoning datasets include RCT Summaries[51], which links randomized controlled trials to PubMed papers, and HypoGen[52], containing approximately 5,500 structured problem-hypothesis pairs from NeurIPS 2023 and ICLR 2024 papers, used for fine-tuning and evaluation.


‌How these resources connect to EXHYTE

E2 (Data Retrieval) is powered by literature and dataset retrieval APIs(Semantic Scholar[1], PubMed[2], arXiv[3], OpenAlex[4], PubChem[6], Amazon EC2[8]) and efficient similarity search tools such as FIASS[53], Tanimoto coefficient[27] etc. 

E3 (Knowledge Assembly) leverages PyPDF2[9], SciPDF[10] for parsing documents, RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter[13] for document chunking, pymupdf4llm[11], S2ORC- doc2json[12] for documents formatting, BLINK[15], NER[16], PL-Marker[17], SciCo[18] for entity extraction and normalization. H2 (Prioritization) draws on similarity metrics (Tanimoto[27], ChemBERTa[54]) and benchmark corpora (e.g., TOMATO- Chem[43], Matbench[40]) to score plausibility and novelty against known neighborhoods. T1–T2 (Test & Refinement) use domain datasets (OC20-S2EF[42], JARVIS- DFT[41], MIMIC-CDM[38], ZINC[35]) to instantiate executable protocols, measure performance with domain metrics, and iterate using empirical feedback.

A typical in silico screening loop begins by validating textual inputs (molecu- lar SMILE string using RDKit[29]) and PyPDF2[9], SciPDF[10], and pymupdf4llm[11] are used for related PDF documents parsing and content extraction sup- ported by structured conversion pipelines such as S2ORC-doc2json[12] and the RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter[13]. Candidate representations are then retrieved through embedding-based similarity search, using FAISS[28] for vector indexing and retrieval, Tanimoto[27] fingerprint similarity or ChemBERTa[54] latent-space cosine sim- ilarity for molecular comparison, and text embeddings generated via models such as Text-Embedding-3[25], SciBERT[22], or BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5[24]. Task-relevant features are extracted and normalized via entity recognition and linking tools including BLINK[15], NER[16], PL-Marker[17], SciCo[18], and ScispaCy[19]. Candidate hypothe- ses or molecular designs are subsequently evaluated on domain-specific benchmark datasets—such as Matbench[40] or MP-20[7] providing quantitative feedback for H2 prioritization and informing iterative refinement under T1–T2. This loop enables EXHYTE systems to continuously integrate retrieval (E2), structured knowledge assembly (E3), and hypothesis ranking (H2) within a unified testing and improvement cycle.

Together, these tools and datasets provide the material on which the EXHYTE strategies operate, enabling systems to move from retrieval and assembly to principled prioritization, testing, and refinement across chemistry, materials, biomedicine, and general scientific reasoning.
